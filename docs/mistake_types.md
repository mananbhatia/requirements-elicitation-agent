Category A: Follow-up Mistakes

Type 1: Fail to probe assumptions
Definition: The consultant accepts a stated or implied assumption the client has made without questioning whether that assumption is justified or complete.
Detection signal: The client states something as obvious or given, and the consultant treats it as settled rather than asking who made that call, why, or what scope it covers.

Type 2: Fail to explore alternatives
Definition: The consultant does not ask about alternative approaches, options, or perspectives when the client presents a decision, constraint, or preference.
Detection signal: The client presents a choice they’ve made or a direction they’re heading, and the consultant accepts it without asking what else was considered, what trade-offs were weighed, or whether other approaches might work.

Type 3: Fail to follow up when needed
Definition: The consultant fails to follow up on something the client said that warranted further inquiry. This covers two cases with the same root cause: the client says something vague or ambiguous and the consultant moves on without asking for specificity, or the client raises something substantive — a new stakeholder, a new problem, a concern — and the consultant moves to a different topic without exploring it.
Detection signal: The client's response contains information that opens a clear line of inquiry, and the consultant's next question does not address it.
Important nuance: Normal topic transitions after a thread has been reasonably explored are not this mistake. The test is whether the consultant ignored something important, not whether they covered everything.

Category B: Question Framing Mistakes

Type 4: Ask a vague or generic question
Definition: The question is too broad, too generic, or lacks sufficient focus for the client to give a specific, useful answer. The question could apply to any company or any domain without modification.
Detection signal: The question contains no reference to anything specific to this client’s situation, domain, or previously shared context.
Important nuance: Opening questions in an interview may be intentionally broad to let the client set direction. A broad opening question is not automatically a mistake — it becomes one when the consultant continues asking broad questions after enough context has been established to ask specific ones. The evaluator must consider the question’s position in the conversation.

Type 5: Ask a question inappropriate to client’s level
Definition: The question uses jargon, technical framing, or asks about things that require knowledge the client does not have given their defined maturity level.
Detection signal: The question contains terms or concepts that require a higher technical maturity than the client has. The evaluator must reference the maturity level description provided in the scenario to make this judgment.
Important nuance: This type is maturity-dependent. The same question can be appropriate for one persona and inappropriate for another. A consultant using a technical term the client has already used in the conversation is not a mistake — the client has demonstrated familiarity with that term.

Type 6: Ask for solutions
Definition: The consultant asks the client to provide or choose a technical solution, rather than proposing an approach and asking for the client’s reaction based on their lived experience.
Detection signal: The question puts the burden of technical decision-making on the client rather than the consultant proposing and testing an approach.
Important nuance: Proposing a solution and gauging the client’s reaction is good consulting practice, not a mistake. Only asking the client to PROVIDE or CHOOSE a solution counts.

Type 7: Bundle distinct topics
Definition: The consultant combines questions about distinct domains or topic areas into a single turn, forcing the client to choose which thread to follow and causing other threads to be dropped.
Detection signal: The question contains two or more sub-questions that address different topic areas in a single turn.
Important nuance: Multiple questions within the SAME topic area are acceptable probing technique, not bundling. The test is whether the sub-questions belong to different domains or the same domain explored from multiple angles.
